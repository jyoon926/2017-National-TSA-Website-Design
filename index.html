The Mission To Mars

Will We Achieve This Goal?

Exploration of Space

There have been and are many ongoing searches for ways to explore our solar system and learn more about the whole universe. On October 4, 1957, the Soviets launched the first ever artificial satellite, Sputnik 1, into space. On July 20, 1969, Astronaut Neil Armstrong took "a giant step for mankind" as he stepped onto the moon. Six Apollo missions were made to explore the moon between 1969 and 1972. On November 13, 1971, the Mariner 9 probe became the first craft to orbit another world, Mars. By the end of the 1970s, the Voyager spacecraft had sent back detailed images of Jupiter and Saturn, their rings, and their moons. Six years later the probe Viking 2 discovered martian frost on the Martian surface. There are many ways to explore the solar system, and these are just a few ways the world has privatized space.

Careers

AVIONICS ENGINEER

Avionic engineers design and program electronic systems on satellites and spaceships. They also provide computer support to all communication, navigation, and guidance systems.

Payload specialists organize and fly a payload in space to it's destination. Payload specialists can be trained to be mission specialists.

PAYLOAD SPECIALIST

MISSION SPECIALIST

Together with the commander and pilot, they plan out crew activities, missions, use of rations, and mange the space equipment. Mission specialist sometimes start of as payload specialists.

Aerospace engineers are vital to the functioning of a ship. They design the control panel, guidance system, computer system, and propulsion system. They also conduct research and experiment, looking at the structural stability and test flight of a ship.

AEROSPACE ENGINEER

CAD OPERATOR

CAD operators provide the guidelines and technical details of the product or structure being built. They draw blueprints, and drawings on the computer for their employer.

Aeronautical engineers work with aircraft. They are involved primarily in designing aircraft and propulsion systems and in studying the aerodynamic performance of aircraft and construction materials. They work with the theory, technology, and practice of flight within the earth's atmosphere.

February 2nd: Eileen Collins became the first female Shuttle pilot.

December: The Galileo probe began transmitting data on Jupiter.

February 1st: The Space Shuttle Columbia broke up on re-entry into the Earth's atmosphere.

September 21st: NASA's Galileo mission ended a 14-year exploration of the solar system's largest planet and its moons with the spacecraft crashing by design into Jupiter at 108,000 mph.

2005

July 3rd: A NASA spacecraft collided with a comet half the size of Manhattan, creating a brilliant cosmic smashup designed to help scientists study life on earth.

July 26th: Space Shuttle Discovery was launched with seven astronauts aboard; this was America's first manned space shot since the 2003 Columbia disaster.

2006

January 15th: NASA spacecraft Stardust returned safely to Earth in a desert near Salt Lake City with the first dust ever collected from a comet.

2007

August 4th: NASA launched its Phoenix Mars Lander.

August 8th: Space Shuttle Endeavour and a crew of seven blasted off with teacher-astronaut Barbara Morgan aboard as a crewmember. Morgan was the first teacher in space since the Challenger disaster in 1986.

2008

January 14th: The NASA space probe Messenger skimmed 124 miles above Mercury.

May 25th: NASA's Phoenix Mars Lander landed safely and began sending images home after a 10-month, 422 million-mile journey. Scientists later reported that Phoenix discovered chunks of ice.

2009

November 13th: NASA scientists announced the discovery of a significant amount of ice in a crater near the moon's South Pole.

2011

July 8th: The space shuttle Atlantis became the last American space shuttle to be launched into space. Mission STS-135 and its 4-member crew brought much-needed supplies and equipment to the International Space Station (ISS).

Space Vehicles and Spacecrafts

The Evolution of Space Vehicles

Space vehicles first started with the mathematical demonstration about how to achieve liftoff using liquid fuels. This was eventually achieved when Robert Goddard launched the first liquid-fueled rocket on March 16, 1926. Later on nearing the end of World War II, Germany launched ballistic missiles respectively known as the A4 and the V-2. The space age then started when Satellite Sputnik became the first satellite to be launched into space on October 4, 1957.

Manned Spacecrafts

The Vostok Program is responsible for starting the production of manned spacecrafts. Mercury was the first manned spacecraft made by the United States. Apollo spacecrafts became the first to be able to reach the moon. The Space Shuttle was developed to build Space Stations such as the ISS. China developed the Shenzhou spacecraft, and it became the third nation to achieve independent human spaceflight capabilities. NASA is currently developing a spacecraft capable of going to Mars, as well as other private spaceflight companies such as SpaceX and Blue Origin. The current manned programs are: Soyuz Program, Shenzhou Program, ISS, and Tiangong-2.

Unmanned Spacecrafts

Most of the unmanned spacecrafts are either satellites or space probes, which are used to study and record different information. However, NASA has launched other unmanned vehicles such as the Mars rovers.

Pros and Cons of Manned Missions to Mars

Introduction

There is a lot of controversy on whether NASA or any other private spaceflight company should invest money and resources on colonizing Mars. There are many advantages, but disadvantages also have to be considered. Should the human race trust our luck and test our skills to embark on a mission of this scale, or should we not risk it, for there are so many things that could go wrong?

Pros

First of all, on the positive side, there is a chance that we could discover new forms of microscopic life on Mars. Especially since there is a possibility that there is flowing water on Mars, the chances are quite high. Another advantage is that it could be a stepping stone for exploring the galaxy. Finding out that the pinnacle of the human race's capability will stop at Earth's orbit would be sad. Also, being able to colonize Mars would inspire the future generations of engineers.

Cons

On the other hand, there are disadvantages that make this decision a lot harder. The biggest reason why we shouldn't invest in another planet, is that there are more significant problems here on Earth. Poverty, global warming, the mass extinction of non-human species, war, gun laws, racism, sexism, a low quality and unequal public educational system, pollution, and destruction of wildlife/habitats are just a fraction of the problems that human race faces. We should be focusing on fixing our home planet rather than invest in another.

Interviews

Interview Questions

Professor Dmitry Savransky at Cornell University

Professor Daniel Selva at Cornell University

Q: Let's start with a very basic question. What do you do in your field of work?

Prof. Savransky: I am a mechanical and aerospace engineer. This field encompasses a huge variety of disciplines, including the study of dynamics and control systems, fluid dynamics, thermodynamics and heat transfer, as well as design and countless application areas. My particular expertise is in dynamics and control systems - the study of how physical objects move and behave in response to various forces, and how to get them to do whatever it is that you need them to do. I am particularly interested in the dynamics of stars, planetary systems (like our solar system)---a field called Celestial Mechanics---as well as the dynamics and control of satellites and spacecraft. Along with my colleagues and my students, I work on many different problems in these areas. Some of these include: helping to plan future space observatories (such as the Hubble Space Telescope, but which are currently being designed); studying how to best figure out how to make multiple spacecraft carry out coordinated tasks (this is referred to as formation flying); and building optical systems (like digital cameras, but slightly more complicated) than can automatically reconfigure and realign themselves to perform different tasks.

Prof. Selva: As you probably know, I'm a professor of aerospace engineering and systems engineering at Cornell. As a professor, there are three aspects of my work: research, teaching, and service. My research is at the intersection of space systems and artificial intelligence. In a nutshell, I develop software tools to help organizations like NASA design better space missions faster. I also participate in actual flight missions. For example, I am on the constellation and instrument science team for the NASA TROPICS mission. In terms of teaching, I teach a class on spacecraft technologies and architectures, and another one on system architecture. Finally, in terms of service, I help professional organizations like IEEE and AIAA organize aerospace conferences. I also serve as the faculty advisor for the Cornell Rocketry Team.

Q: What made you want to be an aeronautical space engineer?

Prof. Savransky: I have always been fascinated by space travel and astronomy. I had the opportunity to study these subjects a little bit in school, and then in much more depth in college, and continued to look for opportunities to learn more and get involved in various projects related to the science and technology of space exploration.

Prof. Selva: I'm not sure -- I've wanted to be an aerospace engineer for as long as I can remember. I think it's probably a mix of factors: the coolness of participating in space exploration, and the challenge of being able to do "rocket science".

Q: What are some tools that you use for your work?

Prof. Savransky: Much of my work begins with just paper and pencil (or a whiteboard). I create models of the systems I wish to study on paper---these are mathematical models and so math and physics are the fundamental tools with which I work. Very quickly, these models become too difficult to solve by hand, and so we turn to computers to help us out. A lot of my time is spent writing programs to solve the particular problems I happen to be working on. Once I am satisfied that I understand how my model of a system works and can be solved in simulation, the final step is to build a physical prototype and test it in the lab. Here, there are many different tools that can be brought to bear. Since a lot of our work has to do with optical systems, I frequently use lasers and other interesting optical devices. One of the coolest things I've ever had a chance to work with is called a deformable mirror. This is a small mirror (the one I used was less than an inch across, although they come in various sizes), that can actually change its shape very quickly. These devices have many uses, but the one I work with is actually used as part of a very complex camera attached to a giant telescope. The deformable mirror is programmed to make shapes that account for the moving atmosphere (like wind blowing over the top of the telescope), which allows us to take much, much clearer images of stars and to even take images of planets orbiting other stars.

Prof. Selva: In order to develop my own optimization, machine learning, and visual analytics tools, I use Matlab, Java, Python, and a few special programming languages such as JESS. I also use astrodynamics tools such as Systems Toolkit.

Q: Do you design any kinds of aircrafts or spacecrafts?

Prof. Savransky: I do not design aircraft. I do take part in the process to design spacecraft - in particular, astronomical space telescopes. As I previously mentioned, aerospace engineering covers an amazing range of disciplines, and all of these are needed to build a spacecraft. It takes a large team of many people with many different specializations and unique skills in order to produce a successful space mission.

Prof. Selva: I design primarily spacecraft, although many years ago I did my Masters thesis in the aerodynamics of jet engines.

Q: What are the main tasks or responsibilities that are required at work?

Prof. Savransky: I am currently an assistant professor (this is the lowest rank of professor at a US university - I started 3 years ago). My job is split fairly evenly between teaching and research. I typically teach two different courses every year (one per semester), and also advise undergraduate and graduate students. For research, I get to propose projects I would like to work on to various government and other agencies. I then work together with my research group (which includes both graduate and undergraduate students) to figure out exactly how to make our proposed ideas into reality.

Prof. Selva: In an academic position, the main tasks are aligned with the roles I mentioned earlier: researcher, teacher, advisor/mentor. Tasks related to research include reading, writing and reviewing grant proposals and scientific papers, developing and testing models and algorithms, running human and computational experiments, and analyzing the data. Tasks related to teaching include preparing and giving lectures, preparing and grading homework and exams, and answering students' questions by email or in office hours. Tasks related to service include serving on various committees, advising students, and others.

Q: What kind of assignments are given to you for work?

Prof. Savransky: One of the wonderful things about this job is that nobody is really giving you assignments (other than which courses you should teach). You have to come up with ideas for interesting things you would like to do, and then convince people who fund research activities that these are worthwhile projects that you and your team are best suited to carry out.

Prof. Selva: In research, the cool thing is that to a certain extent I can define my own assignments. I write proposals in which I propose funding agencies to do a specific thing, such as designing a mission, or creating a new tool or algorithm. If they like it and decide to fund it, then that becomes my assignment. In teaching, typical assignments include teaching a new or existing class or part of it. In service, assignments include organizing meetings and conferences, taking notes and writing reports.

Q: What do you currently think about eventually being able to successfully living on Mars through the work of aerospace?

Prof. Savransky: I think we're still fairly far off from being able to live on Mars, although I wouldn't be surprised if the first manned missions to Mars happened in the next twenty years. In order to create a human colony on Mars, we'll need lots of different professions, not just aerospace engineers, working together. We'll need all kinds of different engineers, biologists, ecologists, and many many others in order to solve all of the problems that'll arise from trying to live on a whole different planet.

Prof. Selva: First of all, I think that being able to live on some other planetary body, probably Mars, is going to be a necessity for humankind to survive as a species. I also see that as an exciting adventure. As to when that will be possible technology, it's hard to predict as it depends on a number of interdependent issues and incentives like technology, NASA budget, climate change, population growth, political cycles and so forth. I think astronauts will land on Mars in the next 15 years, but colonizing Mars will take hundreds of billions of dollars of investments and probably also a change in people's perceptions of the urgency of leaving Earth.

Q: What school would be best if someone wanted to become an aerospace engineer?

Prof. Savransky: There are a lot of great schools out there where you can learn aerospace engineering, and it's nearly impossible to say that one is 'the best'. I'm certainly a big fan of Cornell, and I think that it can easily be called one of the best places to go if you're interested in this field. However, what you really want to consider is what else a school has to offer, and who are the people on the faculty that is doing the things you think are the most exciting. A really great engineer is one who has many varied skills and interests, and the best way to learn what the next big thing will be is to work alongside the people who are trying to create it.

Prof. Selva: There are many options, but perhaps some of the best ones are MIT, Stanford, Caltech, Georgia Tech, Michigan, Purdue, CU Boulder, and of course Cornell.
