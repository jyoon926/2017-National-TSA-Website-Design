Privatizing Space Exploration

There have been and are many ongoing searches for ways to explore our solar system and learn more about the whole universe. On October 4, 1957, the Soviets launched the first ever artificial satellite, Sputnik 1, into space. On July 20, 1969, Astronaut Neil Armstrong took "a giant step for mankind" as he stepped onto the moon. Six Apollo missions were made to explore the moon between 1969 and 1972. On November 13, 1971, the Mariner 9 probe became the first craft to orbit another world, Mars. By the end of the 1970s, the Voyager spacecraft had sent back detailed images of Jupiter and Saturn, their rings, and their moons. Six years later the probe Viking 2 discovered martian frost on the Martian surface. There are many ways to explore the solar system, and these are just a few ways the world has privatized space.

Careers

AVIONICS ENGINEER

Avionic engineers design and program electronic systems on satellites and spaceships. They also provide computer support to all communication, navigation, and guidance systems.

Payload specialists organize and fly a payload in space to it's destination. Payload specialists can be trained to be mission specialists.

PAYLOAD SPECIALIST

MISSION SPECIALIST

Together with the commander and pilot, they plan out crew activities, missions, use of rations, and mange the space equipment. Mission specialist sometimes start of as payload specialists.

Aerospace engineers are vital to the functioning of a ship. They design the control panel, guidance system, computer system, and propulsion system. They also conduct research and experiment, looking at the structural stability and test flight of a ship.

AEROSPACE ENGINEER

CAD OPERATOR

CAD operators provide the guidelines and technical details of the product or structure being built. They draw blueprints, and drawings on the computer for their employer.

Aeronautical engineers work with aircraft. They are involved primarily in designing aircraft and propulsion systems and in studying the aerodynamic performance of aircraft and construction materials. They work with the theory, technology, and practice of flight within the earth's atmosphere.

A Brief History of Space Exploration

1957

October 4th: The Soviet Union launched the first satellite, Sputnik, into space.

November 3rd: The Soviet spacecraft Sputnik 2 was launched with a dog named Laika on board. Laika did not survive the voyage.

1958

January 31st: Explorer 1 was the first satellite launched by the United States when it was sent into orbit on January 31, 1958. It was designed and built by the Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL) of the California Institute of Technology. The satellite was sent aloft from Cape Canaveral in Florida by the Jupiter C rocket that was designed, built, and launched by the Army Ballistic Missile Agency (ABMA) under the direction of Dr. Wernher Von Braun.

1960

August 19th: The Soviet craft Sputnik 5 was launched, carrying the dogs Strelka and Belka. They became the first living beings to survive a trip into space.

1961

April 12th: Russian cosmonaut Yuri Gagarin became the first human in space.

May 5th: Astronaut Alan Shepard became the first American in space.

May 25th: President Kennedy challenged the country to put a man on the moon by the end of the decade.

1962

February 20th: Astronaut John Glenn became the first American in orbit.

June 16th: Valentina Nikolayeva Tereshkova became the first woman in space.

1965

March 19th: While tethered to his spacecraft, cosmonaut Alexi Leonov became the first man to walk in space.

June 3rd: Astronaut Ed White became the first American to walk in space.

July 14th: The spacecraft Mariner 4 transmitted the first pictures of Mars.

1966

February 3rd: The Russian spacecraft Luna 9 became the first spacecraft to land on the moon.

June 2nd: Surveyor 1 became the first American spacecraft to land on the moon.

1967

January 27th: Astronauts Gus Grissom, Ed White, and Roger Chaffee were killed in an accidental fire in a command module on the launch pad.

April 24th: Cosmonaut Vladimir M. Komarov was killed in a crash when the parachute on his Soyuz 1 spacecraft failed to deploy.

1968

September 15th: The Soviet spacecraft Zond 5 was launched and later became the first spacecraft to orbit the moon and return to Earth.

December 21st: Apollo 8 was launched, and later her crewmembers became the first men to orbit the moon.

1969

July 20th: Neil Armstrong and "Buzz" Aldrin became the first men on the moon.

1970

April 11th: Apollo 13 was launched.

September 12th: The Soviet craft Luna 16 was launched and became the first automatic spacecraft to return soil samples of the moon.

November 17th: The Soviet automatic robot Lunokhod 1 landed on the moon with Luna 17.

1973

May 14th: The U.S. launched its first space station, Skylab.

1976

September: The American probe Viking 2 discovered water frost on the Martian surface.

1980

November 13th: Voyager 1 reached Saturn and began transmitting images.

1983

April 4th: The second Space Shuttle, Challenger, was launched.

June 19th: Sally Ride became the first American woman in space on Challenger’s second mission.

August 30th: Guion Bluford became the first African-American in space.

1984

February 3rd: Astronaut Bruce McCandless became the first man to take an untethered space walk.

August 30th: The third Space Shuttle, Discovery, was launched.

October: Kathryn Sullivan became the first American woman to walk in space.

1986

January 24th: Voyager 2 began transmitting images from Uranus.

January 28th: The Space Shuttle Challenger exploded seconds after liftoff.

1989

August: Voyager 2 began transmitting images from Neptune.

1990

August 10th: The Magellan spacecraft began mapping the surface of Venus using radar equipment.

August 24th: The Space Shuttle Discovery deployed the Hubble Space Telescope.

1992

May 7th: The Space Shuttle Endeavor was launched on her maiden voyage.

September 12th: Mae Jemison became the first African-American woman in space.

1993

December: The Space Shuttle Endeavor made the first servicing mission of the Hubble Space Telescope.

1995

February 2nd: Eileen Collins became the first female Shuttle pilot.

December: The Galileo probe began transmitting data on Jupiter.

1997

July 4th: The Mars Pathfinder arrived on Mars and later began transmitting images.

2000

February 14th: The U.S. Near Earth Asteroid Rendezvous (NEAR) spacecraft began transmitting images of the asteroid Eros.

2003

February 1st: The Space Shuttle Columbia broke up on re-entry into the Earth's atmosphere.

August 25th: NASA launched the largest-diameter infrared telescope ever in space, the Spitzer Space Telescope.

September 21st: NASA's Galileo mission ended a 14-year exploration of the solar system's largest planet and its moons with the spacecraft crashing by design into Jupiter at 108,000 mph.

2005

July 3rd: A NASA spacecraft collided with a comet half the size of Manhattan, creating a brilliant cosmic smashup designed to help scientists study life on earth.

July 26th: Space Shuttle Discovery was launched with seven astronauts aboard; this was America's first manned space shot since the 2003 Columbia disaster.

2006

January 15th: NASA spacecraft Stardust returned safely to Earth in a desert near Salt Lake City with the first dust ever collected from a comet.

2007

August 4th: NASA launched its Phoenix Mars Lander.

August 8th: Space Shuttle Endeavour and a crew of seven blasted off with teacher-astronaut Barbara Morgan aboard as a crewmember. Morgan was the first teacher in space since the Challenger disaster in 1986.

2008

January 14th: The NASA space probe Messenger skimmed 124 miles above Mercury.

May 25th: NASA's Phoenix Mars Lander landed safely and began sending images home after a 10-month, 422 million-mile journey. Scientists later reported that Phoenix discovered chunks of ice.

2009

March 6th: The NASA spacecraft Kepler was launched. Its mission is to search for planets outside our solar system, in a distant area of the Milky Way.

June 18th: NASA launched the Lunar Crater Observation and Sensing Satellite, also known as LCROSS. The mission is to confirm the presence or absence of ice on the moon.

November 13th: NASA scientists announced the discovery of a significant amount of ice in a crater near the moon's South Pole.

2011

July 8th: The space shuttle Atlantis became the last American space shuttle to be launched into space. Mission STS-135 and its 4-member crew brought much-needed supplies and equipment to the International Space Station (ISS).

July 16th: NASA's Dawn spacecraft became the first man made craft to orbit an asteroid.

November 26: NASA launched Curiosity, the biggest, best equipped robot ever sent to explore another planet. It will reach Mars in 2012.

2012

August 6th: NASA's Curiosity rover successfully landed on Mars. As large as a car, it carried an array of advanced new instruments and experiments.

2013

September 7th: NASA launched the unmanned LADEE spacecraft from NASA's Wallops Flight Facility in Virginia. It was the U.S. space agency's third lunar probe in five years.

December 24th: NASA astronauts wrapped up successful repairs at the International Space Station after a rare Christmas Eve spacewalk to fix an equipment cooling system.

Space Vehicles and Spacecrafts

The Evolution of Space Vehicles

Space vehicles first started with the mathematical demonstration about how to achieve liftoff using liquid fuels. This was eventually achieved when Robert Goddard launched the first liquid-fueled rocket on March 16, 1926. Later on nearing the end of World War II, Germany launched ballistic missiles respectively known as the A4 and the V-2. The space age then started when Satellite Sputnik became the first satellite to be launched into space on October 4, 1957.

Manned Spacecrafts

The Vostok Program is responsible for starting the production of manned spacecrafts. Mercury was the first manned spacecraft made by the United States. Apollo spacecrafts became the first to be able to reach the moon. The Space Shuttle was developed to build Space Stations such as the ISS. China developed the Shenzhou spacecraft, and it became the third nation to achieve independent human spaceflight capabilities. NASA is currently developing a spacecraft capable of going to Mars, as well as other private spaceflight companies such as SpaceX and Blue Origin. The current manned programs are: Soyuz Program, Shenzhou Program, ISS, and Tiangong-2.

Unmanned Spacecrafts

Most of the unmanned spacecrafts are either satellites or space probes, which are used to study and record different information. However, NASA has launched other unmanned vehicles such as the Mars rovers.

Pros and Cons of Manned Missions to Mars

Introduction

Like the bold journey of Christopher Columbus to the unknown worlds of North America, NASA is set to go on a 21 month manned mission to the surface of Mars by 2030. With this in mind, there are two sides that people seem to align with when discussing a manned mission to another planet like Mars.

Advantages

One side believes that by sending humans to Mars, we are completing yet another huge technological feat while gaining a valuable understanding of life. One of the greatest reasons why these people think this, is because there is a chance that astronomers will discover new forms of life on Mars. There have been many instances where scientists have gotten evidence for possible signs of life. The most obvious are the discovery of ice water, and a conceivable chance of there being liquid water. Water is the best way of looking for life because it is a precursor for it to exist. NASA astronaut Rex Walheim said, "There have been a lot of clues we've seen that there is potentially life on Mars, but the only way to find out is to really go there,"" and he is totally true. There is no way of knowing. Also, Mars has several elements that could be harvested and utilized to expand and support human exploration of Mars and beyond. We could essentially harvest raw materials, construct space vehicles, and launch to other planetary bodies from the surface of Mars without wasting time and resources here on Earth. For example, methane, which is a very common material on Mars, could be used to build plastic based structures. Third, Mars could be a safety planet just in case we destroy this one. Humans have caused many global problems here on Earth, that might prevent us from being able to survive. Global warming, pollution, destruction of wildlife/habitat, overpopulation, and countless more issues, may eventually force us to either die out or move the location to another world. Finally, this mission could be a stepping stone for exploring the universe. Ever since the Apollo missions to the moon in the min-1900s, there hasn't been any revolutionary events or advancements in terms of space exploration. For NASA to be relevant to the needs of society, they must constantly be making advancements that amaze the average citizen. Nobody wants to see that the pinnacle of NASA's capability won't be able to go further than our own moon. After all, who else is going to inspire the future generations of engineers to dream the unimaginable.

Disadvantages

However, there are many downsides that the human race must consider. This side believes that a manned mission is the opposite of what we should do because it will be a challenge that is too hard to take on. Probably the most important reason why we should avoid a mission like this is because it will be too dangerous. It anything bad happens along the way, it would take no less than six months to send a team to rescue the long dead astronauts. Also, there are negative effects in staying in space for that long. Space radiation and microgravity. Space radiation can often cause cancer, cataract formation, and even death. Although scientists are developing materials to diminish incoming radiation and utilize a combination of radioprotectors to limit the effects of ionizing radiation, the level of radiation will sometimes be too high to safely protect an astronaut. Microgravity, on the other hand, can cause muscles to shrink bones to weaken. To cancel out the effects of microgravity, a rigid exercise routine and proper nutrient intake are necessary. Without them, microgravity will most likely have very negative effects on you. Another downside is that the mission will be too costly. Estimates say that it will cost more than 20 million dollars, and that money could go towards a better cause such as fighting the rising national debt, infrastructure, education, and other immediately useful projects. Third, and finally, there are more significant problems here on Earth. Why try to conquer another planet, if we cannot even control one? Things like poverty, war, global warming, and pollution are problems that we should be investing most of our time on. We should be focusing on fixing our home planet than invest in another.

Conclusion

In a nutshell, we don't really have an answer to the question of whether we should invest our resources in a manned mission to Mars or not. There are so many things we need to consider, before settling on a final decision. But back to Christopher Columbus. He, along with his brave crew, embarked on a journey that many thought was impossible. He risked everything that he had including his life so that he can discover the New World. Should we trust our luck and skills and do what Columbus did, or should we not risk it, and instead solve major problems on our home planet, Earth? We will have to wait and find out.
